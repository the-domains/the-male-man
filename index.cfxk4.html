Stop or Delay Dementia

I saw part of an interesting program on a local PBS station recently. It featured a Psychiatrist, Daniel G. Amen

The topic of discussion revolved around maintaining a healthy brain. His central point centered on avoiding or at least forestalling the onset of dementia as we age. His main go-to evidence was brain imaging, observing healthy brain activity versus that of people with Alzheimer’s, repeated concussions or various disorders like ADD.

Several points stood out for their simplicity and novelty. Early on he focused on the importance of how we think and behave now in ways that can sabotage our later health. It was a rather in-depth discussion and I will include source material you can use to learn more.

If you’ve ever read a book on how to maintain a positive mental attitude, you would find that none of what he said was new. What was new was the physical evidence offered as opposed to anecdotal. It’s one thing to say, “I was a sad sack until I learned to think positively. And then I lived happily ever after.” Amen demonstrated, with observable evidence, how our thinking impacts our well being.

He went further, dealing with how our nutrition and activity contribute as well.

The point the jumped out of the screen at me was something so simple that I had to just shake my head and marvel at how the ancients were actually onto something when bleeding with leeches.

We have all heard about how an iron deficiency can be unhealthy. What I never knew was the effect of too much iron were, aside from very uncomfortable stools.

One effect of too much iron is to contribute to problems you don’t know about until much later in life. It can contribute to diminished brain activity over time.

One solution? Give blood. We should do so regularly. My mother was a regular blood donor and told me it was a healthy practice but could never articulate why. It turns out that this is one of the best ways to deal with unhealthy iron levels in our bodies.

When you think of it that way, hospitals should never have a shortage of blood. We should be going in and having our oil changed every time we bring the car in for one. When the body regenerates the lost cells and hemoglobin, it does so without the added iron content. It reduces all pollutants in that way.

As I was telling my wife the medieval doctors were onto something with that, Doctor Amen showed an aquarium of leeches and said the same thing. There were probably lots of other heavy metals and pollutants in the body that bleeding helped people with back then. People drank from lead vessels and ate from porous, dirty pottery. Metal objects were far softer and more crude than todays alloys. Blacksmiths never used any protection. The physician knew something good was happening with leeching and blood-letting. They even tried to label the effects – good and bad “humors” – but there was clearly something beneficial happening.

So from now on, when you think of giving blood, don’t think of it as a chore or generosity on your part. Consider it a part of a larger plan to keep yourself healthy well into your golden years. Everything we do now to maintain our health reduces the risk of us becoming a burden to our children later in life. So think of blood donation as a selfish activity. There is NOTHING wrong with that.

Amen went on to list supplements that are known to help us maintain our bodies and brains over the long term. He talked about Omega 3 fatty acids, Ginko and probiotics among other things. Again I will provide some sources for you later.

If that sounds like alternative medicine, a term I ‘ve been known to scoff at over the years, I will point out that Daniel Amen is a psychiatrist (provable medicine) as opposed to a psychologist (a sometimes dubious discipline). Amen bases his information on measurable results. The difference lies in the following riddle.

Q: What do you call alternative medicine that actually works?

A: Medicine.

So far, the lion’s share of alternative medicine remains just that. But don’t discount those supplements and non-chemical practices that are proven to be beneficial.
